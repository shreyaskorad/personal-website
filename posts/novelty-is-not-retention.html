Novelty is not retention

April 15, 2026 · 2 min read · gamification, modern l&d, behaviour science, systems

Points, badges, and streaks can create an early spike. Long-term retention usually depends on what happens after the novelty fades.

Longitudinal research keeps finding the same pattern: gamification often works as an onboarding device before it works as a retention system. Koivisto and Hamari found that in Fitocracy, perceived usefulness, enjoyment, and playfulness tended to diminish with time using the service, which suggests a real novelty effect rather than durable motivation by default.

Tsay and colleagues saw the same risk in a multi-year gamified learning system. Novelty helped bring learners in, but sustained engagement only improved once the design was iterated around clearer narrative, better onboarding, and more meaningful learning value beyond points, badges, and leaderboards.

The better question is not whether gamification creates a spike. It is whether the system still feels useful and self-directed after the first few weeks. Deci, Koestner, and Ryan showed long ago that rewards experienced as controlling can undermine intrinsic motivation, which is exactly why shallow reward layers often flatten out.

There is a more durable path. Altmeyer and colleagues found that over 275 days, gamification increased participation more when the elements were matched to users instead of treated as one-size-fits-all decoration. In practice, that means designing progression, feedback, and challenge around real user motives instead of hoping badges will carry a broken process.

If removing the points would collapse the behavior, you did not build retention. You rented attention for the novelty window.
