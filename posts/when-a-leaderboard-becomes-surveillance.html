When a leaderboard becomes surveillance

April 15, 2026 · 2 min read · gamification, modern l&d, behaviour science, work

A leaderboard can lift effort for a simple task. In workplace learning and other knowledge work, it often does the opposite: it raises stress, narrows attention, and strips away the autonomy that makes motivation sustainable.

The evidence on motivation is much harsher than most gamification decks admit. Deci, Koestner, and Ryan's classic meta-analysis found that rewards experienced as controlling can undermine intrinsic motivation, especially when the point of the system shifts from meaningful progress to external pressure.

That matters at work because performance is not just about movement. Baard, Deci, and Ryan found that employees perform better when their work supports autonomy, competence, and relatedness. In workplace gamification research, Mitchell and colleagues found the same pattern: intrinsic motivation and need satisfaction predict stronger behavioural intention, while external regulation erodes autonomy and competence.

The monitoring literature points in the same direction. A broad Frontiers review on electronic performance monitoring found more consistent evidence for stress, lower satisfaction, and lower trust than for durable motivation gains, especially as monitoring becomes more invasive or more individualised.

So if you are designing a learning system, use dashboards for reflection, coaching, and visible progress, not public pressure. Let people compare against their own baseline, show quality not just volume, and make recognition feel informational rather than controlling. If the board feels like an electronic whip, the issue is not adoption. It is design.

If a learning experience needs public pressure to stay alive, the design problem is upstream. Build for autonomy, competence, and useful feedback first, then add game mechanics only where they amplify those conditions.
