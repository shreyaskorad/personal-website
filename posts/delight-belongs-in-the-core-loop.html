Delight belongs in the core loop

April 15, 2026 · 2 min read · product, behaviour science, systems, work

Teams often treat delight as polish for after the functional work is done. In practice, delight changes how the product is experienced during use, which is why it has to live inside the core loop.

Marc Hassenzahl and colleagues argued this two decades ago: joy of use is not just the natural result of usability, and it is not something designers can paste on top as superficial decoration. It emerges from the interaction between pragmatic quality and hedonic quality inside the experience itself.

That is why late-stage delight efforts usually feel thin. In goal mode, users care about both pragmatic and hedonic quality, but in activity mode appeal can be driven solely by hedonic quality. If the product is emotionally flat while people are using it, the team cannot repair that with a few cheerful details at the end.

Hassenzahl also argues that products are more stably appreciated when they connect to the self, not just to task completion. That is a much stronger design bar than adding a surprise animation or a playful color once the backlog is clear.

Desmet's work on product pleasure makes the same point from another angle: positive product emotions include surprise, fascination, inspiration, respect, and confidence. Those emotions come from how the product responds, what it enables, and what it makes people feel during the journey. Delight is not a layer. It is a property of the loop.

If delight only appears after the task is complete, it is probably decoration. Real delight changes the experience while the user is still inside it.
