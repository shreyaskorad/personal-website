Effort isn't the problem. Your learning system is

April 16, 2026 · 2 min read · modern l&d, behaviour science, learning, data and analytics

If someone works hard and still fails, do not label it a motivation problem. In most learning systems, the default methods are passive and low yield. Fix the system, and effort starts converting into capability.

A learning designer shared a painful lesson: he failed about half his courses even while putting in effort, because his methods were trash. He was doing what many learners do by default: rereading, highlighting, and cramming the night before.

Modern L&D repeats the same mistake at scale. We ship more content, demand more effort, and then act surprised when results are flat. But the evidence is clear: some study techniques are consistently high utility (practice testing and distributed practice), while others learners gravitate toward (rereading and highlighting) are low utility.

Retrieval practice is a simple example. When you force recall, you strengthen memory and you expose what is missing. Karpicke and Roediger showed that repeated retrieval produced much better long term retention than repeated studying, even when learners believed studying felt more effective.

Spacing is the second lever. A meta-analysis by Cepeda and colleagues found distributed practice reliably improves memory across tasks and timescales. So here is a 7 day move: pick one job critical skill, write 6 to 10 retrieval prompts, schedule three short checks across the week, and measure completion and accuracy. Track retrieval reps, not minutes watched. If you want help rebuilding your learning system around these loops, reach out.

Stop blaming effort. Make the effective method the default.
