Why activity metrics are not evidence of impact

April 17, 2026 · 2 min read · data-and-analytics, learning-design, work

Measurement becomes useful when it sharpens the next intervention, not when it simply reports activity. The better test is simple: does the design change the next decision in real work?

Teams care about the topic, but the surrounding systems still produce activity without durable capability, better decisions, or visible business impact.

The mechanism is not richer reporting. It is whether a metric changes the next coaching or design move.

Borrow one method from recent evidence, run it in one team next week, and keep it only if behavior changes in your workflow.

The source points away from reporting and toward intervention: the metric matters only if it changes the next design, coaching, or management move.

The next design question is whether the mechanism survives inside a real workflow with visible stakes, not just inside the intervention itself.

Across learning science, behavior science, organizational design, cognitive psychology, the same pattern shows up. The intervention matters only if it changes behavior around a live decision.

Grossman & Salas (2011) matters here because counters the habit of mistaking activity metrics for transfer.

Hattie & Timperley (2007) matters here because strong frame for feedback that sharpens the next move.

The tradeoff is clarity versus theater: richer dashboards can either sharpen decisions or distract teams into managing the metric itself.

The broader literature also warns that results depend heavily on context, incentive mix, and implementation details rather than the mechanic alone. ([2]).

Choose one metric that should change the next coaching move. Delete the metrics that cannot explain a better intervention.

Michie, van Stralen, & West (2011) matters here because helps tie metrics back to target behavior.

The useful test is not whether the intervention looks engaging. It is whether the next decision gets sharper.
