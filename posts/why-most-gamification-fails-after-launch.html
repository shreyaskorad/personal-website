Why most gamification fails after launch

April 16, 2026 · 2 min read · learning-design, gamification, behaviour-science

Gamification fails after launch when you reward activity instead of decisions. You get completion, not capability.

Leaderboards and points work when they pay for the next hard rep. They fail when they pay for finishing.

Attach reward to clicks and learners optimise for clicks.

Borrow one method from recent evidence, run it in one team next week, and keep it only if behavior changes in your workflow.

A support ops lead ships an onboarding game: points for watching videos, Slack leaderboard for ‘top learners’.

Week 1: completion spikes. Week 6: QA audits show the same ticket mistakes, and chat turns into ‘what’s the fastest way to get points?’.

Move rewards onto retrieval checks and scenario attempts, and completion will drop. That drop is signal, not failure.

Reward the decision you care about: 3–5 choices that predict performance. Points follow judgment, not time spent.

Add cues (weekly 5‑minute challenge), instant feedback, and spaced retries. Unlock a mentor hint only after a failed attempt.

Track (1) scenario attempt rate and (2) one workflow outcome: error rate, re‑opens, or time‑to‑first‑correct.

Owner: learning lead. First step: pick one workflow decision, write 3 short scenarios, and score attempts (not minutes). Review in 14 days: did attempts rise and did the workflow metric move? This comes back to activity.
