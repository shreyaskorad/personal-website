Design thinking is not retention

April 16, 2026 · 2 min read · modern l&d, behaviour science, gamification, systems

Design thinking can help you ship something usable and intuitive. It does not guarantee people will come back. Retention needs a behaviour model.

In Professor Game episode 438, Joris Beerda put words to a pattern many teams avoid naming. Design thinking can create inclusive, intuitive experiences, yet the product still fails to earn repeat use. The workshop feels productive. The metrics stay flat.

That is because retention is not a UX property. It is a repeated action in a real context. If you cannot name the single behaviour you want repeated, you cannot design for it. And if you cannot explain why it should repeat next week, the system will rely on novelty, guilt, or manager pressure.

I like COM-B as a fast diagnostic. People repeat behaviour when Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation line up. Design thinking often helps with capability and ease. It is weaker at specifying the motivational engine and the feedback loop that makes progress feel real.

Self-determination theory makes this even more concrete. If the experience supports autonomy, competence, and relatedness, effort feels self-directed and worth repeating. If it feels controlling or disconnected from real work, it becomes another tab people stop opening.

If you want a practical move in seven days, run a retention-first design audit. Pick one repeat action that matters, instrument it, and redesign the smallest loop that makes a learner feel competent after one session. If you want help running that audit for your learning product or adoption program, reach out.

Good design is necessary. Retention is earned when the system makes the next repeat action feel obvious and worthwhile.
