I took the long way into learning design. It made all the difference.

Most instructional designers came up through education or HR. I came through advertising, gamification, and a obsession with why some experiences captivate us while others fade from memory.

I started my career running a marketing and advertising company. The work was all about one thing: capturing attention. In a world of infinite distractions, how do you make someone stop, notice, and actually care about what you're saying?

That question led me to gamification. Not the shallow "slap some badges on it" variety, but the deep mechanics that make experiences genuinely compelling. I became fascinated by motivation: why we engage with some things obsessively and abandon others instantly. What makes a game feel impossible to put down? What makes learning feel like a chore?

The more I explored, the more I realized these weren't separate questions. The principles that make games engaging are the same principles that make learning stick. Challenge. Feedback. Progress. Meaning. The difference is in how, and whether, we apply them.

That realization became my bridge into learning and development, where I've spent the last 7+ years applying everything I learned about attention, engagement, and behavior change to the challenge of helping people actually learn.

What I actually believe about learning

Here's the uncomfortable truth about most corporate training: it's designed for the organization, not the learner. It measures completion, not competence. It prioritizes coverage over understanding. And then everyone wonders why nothing changes.

I believe in a different approach.

Learning should feel good

Not in a "fun and games" way, but in the way that mastering something difficult feels good. People learn what they engage with. If your training feels like a chore, that's a design problem, not a learner problem.

Engagement without outcomes is entertainment

Interactivity, gamification, storytelling: these are powerful tools. But they're tools, not goals. If learners are engaged but can't do anything new afterward, you've built a game, not a learning experience.

The science matters

Spaced practice. Retrieval. Scenario-based learning. Cognitive load management. We know more than ever about how people actually learn. Most training ignores it entirely.

Design is problem-solving

Before asking "what should the course look like," I want to know: What do people need to be able to do? What's stopping them? Sometimes the answer isn't training at all.

What makes my perspective different

Most learning designers fall into one of two camps. There are the creative types, strong on storytelling, visuals, and engagement, but less rigorous about learning science and measurement. And there are the analytical types, meticulous about objectives and assessments, but the experiences they create feel clinical and forgettable.

My unconventional path means I don't fit neatly into either camp.

From advertising, I learned to think like a creative director: How do you make something that cuts through the noise? That people actually want to spend time with? That evokes emotion and sticks in memory?

From gamification, I learned to think like a game designer: How do you create sustained motivation? How do you make challenge feel rewarding instead of frustrating? How do you design systems that people choose to engage with?

From L&D, I learned to think like a strategist: How do you connect learning to business outcomes? How do you measure what matters? How do you design for behavior change, not just knowledge transfer?

I bring a creative director's eye for engagement, a game designer's understanding of motivation, and an instructional designer's commitment to outcomes. That combination is rare, and it shapes everything I create.

What I'm most interested in

I'm particularly drawn to problems everyone else finds boring. Compliance training. Mandatory courses. The content that employees click through as fast as possible just to check a box.

There's something deeply satisfying about taking material that everyone dreads and finding a way to make it genuinely useful, maybe even engaging. If you can make ethics training feel relevant, you can make anything work.

I'm also fascinated by the gap between what we know and what we do. Learning science has made enormous strides in understanding how people actually learn, yet most training programs ignore the research entirely. That gap is where I spend a lot of my thinking.

Beyond the work

When I'm not designing learning experiences, I'm usually exploring how other fields solve similar problems. Game design. UX research. Behavioral economics. Narrative theory. The best ideas often come from unexpected places, and the boundaries between disciplines are more porous than we pretend.

I also believe in learning by doing, which means I'm usually tinkering with something. Testing a new approach. Breaking things. Figuring out what works by trying it.
